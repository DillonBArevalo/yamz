Metadictionary guidelines

Metadata and data

Metadata is structured information for describing things we care about. It looks like text with extra punctuation to mark different regions, and elements and values within those regions. That structure makes the information readable by software, which in turn helps us organize, manage, analyze, and discover things we care about.

Real computational power comes when there is wide agreement on metadata terms – on element names and the values they take on. The goal of YAMZ is to support finding, creating, and achieving community accord on high-quality terms. These are the terms that you and others will use to create high-quality metadata. Unlike a regular dictionary, the metadictionary is about terms used to create structured (machine-readable) information.

The metadictionary supports data too. Like metadata, data is structured information, but with a probable shift in emphasis away from being about a particular thing. Some people claim there's no important difference between data and metadata.

Things and assertions about them

Metadata looks like a group of assertions about a particular thing, such as a document. For example, metadata may assert that a given document's title is X, it's author Y, and it's publication date Z.

Term definitions often refer to the thing being described with a word like "thing", "resource", or "object", eg, a title could be "A name given to the resource". Which exact thing is being referred to cannot be found in a term definition. Instead it is revealed in metadata values or by context; for example, metadata inside an image file often describes the image elsewhere in the same file.

Elements and values

Lots of terms define metadata elements, which are small categories of information, such as "title", "author", or "response". An element might also be a structured statement that itself contains other elements; for example, a "location" that contains "latitude" and "longitude". Other elements are meant to take on metadata values, for example, pieces of text or personal names.

Terms also help with metadata values, especially when they are limited in some way. For example, a "response" element might be one of "yes", "no", or "maybe". A "count" element might take an infinite number of values, but always restricted to "integer".

What the metadictionary is not

YAMZ is not

  • ... an encyclopedia. A definition is approximately one sentence, not an essay.
  • ... a grammar. Whether a term is required or optional is out of scope. A term is like a proposed nano-standard, and its definition may link out to external documents specifying rules for given communities.
  • ... a regular dictionary. While there appear to be many natural language terms, they are actually formal jargon inspired by natural language. YAMZ should only contain terms that directly support machine-readable descriptions and the metadictionary itself.
  • ... a bunch of sub-vocabularies. If your working group in the XYZ domain wants its own version of thingummy, resist the temptation to create XYZ_thingummy, or similarly making the domain part of the term. Instead consider creating thingummy and tagging it #XYZ so your group can narrow its focus to #XYZ terms on demand. It is a feature that YAMZ is ultimately one large, flat namespace with no domain borders.

Term parts

Terms consist of several parts: a term string, definition, unique concept identifier, example section, and comments section.

Term string

A term string (or label) is one or more words indicating how we speak about the concept being defined.

  • A term string should contain only letters, digits, hyphens, and underscores. If it has more than one word, exactly one space separates each word.
  • Although definitions are in English, the term string may be derived from any natural language. Whatever its origin, consideration should be given to making it easy to spell, pronounce, and use in English sentences.
  • Prefer creating a term in the singular form, and in such a way that the plural form need not also be defined. This approach is often easier, clearer, and more flexible than creating a term in the plural form.
  • A term string need not be unique in the metadictionary, but consider carefully if you want to re-use an existing string. Either way, each term gets a unique and persistent concept identifier when it is first created. Two terms with the same term string may be meant to compete with or to complement each other.

Term definition

With the term string established, your term definition should consist of two main parts (see [1] and [2]):

  • The class of thing to which the term belongs, followed by
  • differentiating characteristics that distinguish it from all others of its class.
In particular, a definition (in the singular form) should start with the article "A" or "The". As an example, for the term "water",
A clear liquid made up of hydrogen and oxygen molecules.
the class is "clear liquid". If the term string is an acronym, expand it inside parentheses (using capital letters to delineate the acronym), at the very beginning of the definition. As an example, for the term "ABC",
(Apple Bovine Cooling) A treatment for overheated cows involving an ointment extracted from apples.

Referencing terms, tags, and external links

When term definitions, examples, and comments refer to words and concepts defined in YAMZ and elsewhere, you can create actionable links to them with special notation. When you create or edit text in these parts of a term, be aware of constructs that look like 

#{t: <term string> | <concept identifier>}
#{g: <tag string> | <concept identifier>}
#{k: <link string> | <URL/web address>}
&foo (which is shorthand for) #{t: foo}
#foo (which is shorthand for) #{g: foo}
